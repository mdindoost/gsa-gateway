# MMI Workshop Series — Knowledge Base

## Q: What is the MMI Workshop?
**A:** The MultiMedia Intelligence Workshop Series (MMI) is an annual research event hosted at NJIT (New Jersey Institute of Technology). It brings together students, faculty, and international researchers working at the intersection of multimedia, artificial intelligence, and multimodal learning. Each edition is organized around a focused theme and features invited talks from leading researchers worldwide. Topics include multimedia retrieval, multimodal reasoning, vision-language systems, trustworthy AI, information access, and human-centered computing. It is co-organized by the NJIT Computer Science Department and the Graduate Student Association (GSA).

## Q: Who is Prof. Lesia Semenova?
**A:** Prof. Lesia Semenova is an Assistant Professor of Computer Science at Rutgers University, where she leads a research group that advances the foundations, algorithms, and applied practice for safe, trustworthy, and interpretable AI through model and representation multiplicity. Her work formalizes the Rashomon Effect — the existence of many equally accurate but behaviorally different models — to move the field beyond a single-model mindset. By characterizing Rashomon sets (sets of near-optimal models), she develops methods to navigate these spaces and identify models satisfying additional desiderata such as interpretability or robustness. This approach leverages model diversity to enable new algorithmic tools for robust recourse, personalized alignment, and stable decision-making in high-stakes fields like healthcare and public policy. She is extending these ideas to foundation models and LLMs, where multiplicity arises through internal representations and reasoning paths.

Before joining Rutgers, she was a postdoctoral researcher at Microsoft Research (NYC). She received her Ph.D. in Computer Science from Duke University. ## Q: What did Prof. Shin'ichi Satoh present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Shin'ichi Satoh presented "Biases in Machine Learning and AI" at MMI 2026 (Day 2, May 22, 2026, 11:45 AM–12:30 PM). Modern machine learning algorithms effectively find specific patterns from training data. Object detectors learn visual appearances of objects with large variations in pose, lighting, and scale; face recognition systems learn appearances of individuals; segmentation algorithms learn appearances of interiors and boundaries. These specific patterns are essentially biases in training data, and in this sense, machine learning is very good at finding biases. However, biases may cause problems in AI in general — AI may mistakenly correlate criminal rates or income level with attributes such as gender, living places, or alma mater, or may have significantly poor classification performance for specific demographic groups, causing severely negative societal impact. The talk covered actual biases found in machine learning, an attempt to mitigate biases by fine-tuning using unlabeled data, and bias detection in black-box machine learning algorithms with limited access.

## Q: What did Prof. Michael E. Houle present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Michael E. Houle presented "Dimensionality-Aware Analysis of Local Intrinsic Structure in Multimedia Data" at MMI 2026 (Day 1, May 21, 2026, 11:00–11:45 AM). The talk presented a dimensionality-aware approach to data analysis based on Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID), a framework for describing the local complexity of high-dimensional data. Unlike global notions of dimension, LID is defined through the growth rate of neighborhood probability mass, and is equivalent to indiscriminability — the difficulty of distinguishing nearby points. This formulation shows that local distributions can be described in terms of both density and dimensionality, connecting ideas from statistics, machine learning, and similarity search. The talk reviewed practical methods for estimating LID and their use in multimedia applications such as representation learning and outlier detection. It highlighted challenges in deep models where locality must be balanced against computational constraints, and discussed how density-ratio approaches can be adapted to account for variation in intrinsic dimension across data. The talk emphasized that accounting for local dimensional structure leads to more reliable analysis of complex multimedia data.

## Q: What did Prof. Zining Zhu present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Zining Zhu presented "Mechanistic Interpretation and Reasoning AI" at MMI 2026 (Day 1, May 21, 2026, 11:45 AM–12:30 PM). In recent years, AI models have shown strong capabilities in agentic reasoning across multiple media. Despite their promise, it has been challenging to control the reasoning procedure due to the opaque nature of the models. Recent research attempts to explain the reasons behind these behaviors by examining the parameters, from layers to neurons and SAE features. Leveraging these findings, researchers also try to improve reasoning behavior, improving honesty and efficiency. The talk presented several works along these directions and discussed intuitions regarding the explainability and controllability of AI reasoning systems.

## Q: What did Prof. Vivek K. Singh present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Vivek K. Singh presented "How modalities impact safety in information seeking" at MMI 2026 (Day 1, May 21, 2026, 1:30–2:15 PM). Access to reliable health information is critical, especially in vulnerable moments when misleading or unsafe guidance can cause real harm. As AI systems increasingly mediate this access, their ability to resist manipulation and prevent harmful outputs becomes essential for public safety. Equity remains central because uneven protections can expose some users to greater risk than others. The talk examined how modality shapes these outcomes. Modality refers to the forms of expression that mediate interaction between users and large language models, including language choice, transliteration, emojis, and poetic structure. The talk discussed how these forms can shift how safety mechanisms interpret and respond to queries, sometimes weakening protections, and argued that modality is a key but understudied factor in shaping risk and equitable access to information.

## Q: What did Prof. Ping Wang present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Ping Wang presented "Towards Reasoning-Augmented Natural Language Querying for Domain-Specific NoSQL Databases" at MMI 2026 (Day 1, May 21, 2026, 2:15–3:00 PM). Efficient retrieval of critical information from large-scale healthcare databases remains a significant challenge due to the complexity and heterogeneity of medical data. Existing natural language querying (NLQ) research has primarily focused on relational databases, whose rigid data schemas and limited support for full-text search restrict their ability to effectively manage large volumes of unstructured and semi-structured healthcare data. The talk shifted focus to NoSQL databases and investigated the potential of NLQ in this setting, presenting a systematic study spanning dataset construction, model design, controllable reasoning, complexity modeling, and comprehensive evaluation. The work formally defines NLQ over NoSQL databases and introduces an initial two-stage controllable framework. It further advances controllable reasoning through InstructEx, a staged reasoning approach that analyzes and decomposes user intent and generates executable query structures under explicit constraints. To address query efficiency challenges, a query complexity taxonomy and the efficiency-aware optimization framework RACE-ESQ are introduced, dynamically adapting retrieval behavior based on query complexity, establishing a unified framework for reliable, controllable, and efficient NLQ over large-scale healthcare NoSQL databases.

## Q: What did Prof. Cathal Gurrin present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Cathal Gurrin delivered the Day 2 Keynote at MMI 2026 (May 22, 2026, 9:30–10:40 AM) titled "Lifelogs and Personal Data. An Unsolved Challenge." Lifelogging refers to the capture, storage, and access of large archives of personal media gathered over an extended period of time. In the most well-known form, lifelogs are gathered passively using devices such as wearable cameras and other hardware and software sensors. Over the past decade, significant progress has been made in organizing and accessing such lifelogs, turning them from large data repositories into impactful information systems supporting a wide variety of access mechanisms with the promise of becoming always-on daily life assistants. The talk reviewed how modern lifelog retrieval systems work and explored the challenge of obtaining sufficient lifelog data to develop and evaluate such systems. It examined the latest lifelog-related datasets being created and concluded with a suggestion of what an ideal next-generation dataset would look like.

## Q: What is MARCuS?
**A:** MARCuS (Multi-Modal AR Cube Solver) is a HoloLens-based augmented reality system developed to demonstrate Perceptual Task Guidance — an AR paradigm in which the system uses visual perception, task modeling, language grounding, user modeling, and spatial communication to assist humans during complex physical tasks. MARCuS combines perception-enabled AR, multimodal guidance, adaptive visual feedback, and state verification. It was presented as a case study by Prof. Enrique Dunn (Stevens Institute of Technology) in his MMI 2026 talk "Augmented Reality for Perceptual Task Guidance."

## Q: What did Prof. Enrique Dunn present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Enrique Dunn presented "Augmented Reality for Perceptual Task Guidance" at MMI 2026 (Day 2, May 22, 2026, 11:00–11:45 AM). Artificial intelligence, computer vision, and augmented reality are converging to create interactive systems that can perceive the physical world, reason about human activity, and deliver context-sensitive guidance. The talk revisited the foundations and evolution of augmented and extended reality, from early head-mounted displays and virtual fixtures to contemporary spatial computing platforms, situated within broader advances in computer vision and AI. The central focus was Perceptual Task Guidance, an emerging paradigm in which AR systems use visual perception, task modeling, language grounding, user modeling, and spatial communication to assist humans during complex physical tasks. The talk discussed the technical goals of building an AR apparatus capable of grounded physical mapping, automated task understanding, responsive interaction, customization, and generalization across tasks and users. A case study, MARCuS (Multi-Modal AR Cube Solver), demonstrated these ideas through a HoloLens-based system combining perception-enabled AR, multimodal guidance, adaptive visual feedback, and state verification.

## Q: What did Prof. Yan Sun present at MMI 2026?
**A:** Prof. Yan Sun presented "Uncertainty Quantification for Generative Models" at MMI 2026 (Day 2, May 22, 2026, 1:30–2:15 PM). Uncertainty quantification plays a major role in ensuring the reliability of traditional machine learning systems. The talk presented two applications of uncertainty quantification techniques in modern generative models. The first part focused on statistical inference for generative model evaluation — specifically assessing whether one model performs better than another with statistical confidence. By leveraging a relative KL divergence, an estimator is provided that is asymptotically normal, enabling valid inference for model comparison applicable to auto-encoders, diffusion models, and auto-regressive language models. The second part focused on reasoning language models. When presented with incomplete or ambiguous questions, these models may waste computational resources by generating unnecessarily long reasoning traces. The talk developed methods that extract uncertainty signals from the model's reasoning traces to determine when reasoning should stop under high uncertainty, potentially reducing computational cost and enabling models to request clarification when needed.

Machine learning models are increasingly used in high-stakes domains such as medicine, but their predictions and explanations are often evaluated one model at a time. This can be misleading: in many learning problems, multiple models achieve similar predictive performance while relying on different features, concepts, or internal representations. This phenomenon, known as the Rashomon Effect, raises a central question for trustworthy AI: which conclusions are stable across many good models, and which are artifacts of a particular training run or modeling choice? The talk discussed recent work on interpretability and model multiplicity, showing how reasoning over sets of near-optimal models can support trustworthy AI goals, with an emphasis on interpretability, stability, and high-stakes decision support in domains such as healthcare and public policy.
